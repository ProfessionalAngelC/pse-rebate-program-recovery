# Cross-Reference: PSE Rebate Program Recovery ↔ Operations Command Center

**Purpose:** State precisely how these two projects relate — no more and no less than the evidence supports.

---

## The real timeline

These two projects are related in subject matter, but they were not planned together, and they don't cover the same period of time.

1. **2024** — I took over the PSE rebate program directly, after it had been losing money under prior management. I built the documentation and photo-compliance process from scratch and processed the majority of the paperwork myself.
2. **2025–2026** — I trained a team to take over processing, then consolidated it to a single dedicated processor.
3. **Separately, while monitoring the now-stabilized PSE program**, I noticed a recurring pattern: estimators creating job profiles in the field (rather than the office) were sometimes capturing incomplete or incorrect information, which caused downstream errors — including some PSE documentation issues. This observation led to a second, independent initiative: **Operations Command Center**, which redesigned the workflow so the office creates and standardizes job profiles, with a structured review step — including a PSE-readiness checkpoint — before an estimate goes to the customer.

Operations Command Center was not planned as a "phase two" of the PSE recovery. It was a separate discovery, made later, while working within an already-healthy program.

## What this project's data can actually say about that connection

This PSE analysis — built afterward, in 2026, from three years of the program's own real records — gives that earlier decision concrete supporting evidence:

**9 of the 13 documented PSE rebate losses in this dataset (69.2%) trace directly to estimator-side gaps** — missed eligibility checks, duplicate claims, and incorrect job details (like heat source) captured at the point a job was created. These are exactly the category of error Operations Command Center's review checkpoint was built to catch.

## What this project's data cannot say

**This dataset cannot serve as a before/after test of whether Operations Command Center's fix actually worked.** Every job in this three-year dataset was created in the field — there is no office-created comparison population anywhere in it, because the shift to office-created profiles happened as a separate initiative outside the timeframe this data covers. A statistical field-vs-office comparison isn't possible here, and I'm not claiming one.

What this data does support is narrower and, I think, more honest: **the underlying problem Operations Command Center addresses was real, and it had a measurable, itemized dollar cost within this program specifically.** That's a different claim than "the fix worked," and the difference matters.

## Summary

| Claim | Supported by this data? |
|---|---|
| Estimator-side errors caused real, quantifiable PSE rebate losses | ✅ Yes — 9 of 13 records, itemized |
| The 2026 rise in loss rate is driven by estimators, not processing | ✅ Yes — 4 of 5 2026 losses estimator-attributable, zero processor-attributed |
| Office-created profiles have a lower error rate than field-created ones | ❌ No — no comparison population exists in this dataset |
| Operations Command Center's fix reduced errors | ❌ No — outside the scope of this data; would require a separate before/after study using data from after that fix went live |
